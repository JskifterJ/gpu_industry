① ASML — The First Chokepoint
ASML ships ~50–55 High-NA EUV machines per year globally — there is no other supplier. Each machine costs ~$380M and requires 12–18 months of lead time. Sub-3nm lithography capacity is therefore physically capped, and cannot be scaled in any window shorter than a decade.
② TSMC — The Second Chokepoint
TSMC is the sole sub-3nm GPU foundry in production. H100/H200/B200 — all fabbed at TSMC on 4N/3NP nodes. Foundry expansion (CoWoS packaging for HBM) has a multi-year ramp. No competitor has reached equivalent yield at these nodes. Even NVIDIA is capacity-constrained by what TSMC can produce.
③ NVIDIA — Allocation Dynamics
Priority allocation flows to hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, GCP) and select government partners first — through NVIDIA Preferred Partner (NPN) agreements and direct government deals. Every new GPU generation is supply-constrained for 18–24 months post-launch — structurally, not accidentally. Smaller operators and new geographies wait.

Context Window Grows → Concurrent Users Collapse.
KV cache grows linearly with context length. On a fixed-memory GPU, every 10× increase in context = 10× fewer concurrent requests.

Why Context Size is the Hardest Hardware Problem
Memory Capacity Ceiling
A 128k context session on LLaMA 70B occupies ~320MB of HBM — more than 4× the memory of a 2k session. This is why the H200 upgrade from H100 was so impactful: 141GB vs 80GB nearly doubles the number of concurrent long-context requests.
Bandwidth Ceiling
For long-context autoregressive decode, the GPU must stream the entire KV cache from HBM on every generated token. At 128k context, this is 320MB moved per token per user. The H200's 4.8 TB/s bandwidth allows ~15k tokens/s vs ~10k on H100 for the same context size.
Commercial Implication
Long-context pricing is non-linear. OpenAI charges 5× more per token at 128k context vs 8k. This reflects real hardware costs: you are paying for reserved KV cache slots, not just compute. Providers must price by context × concurrency, not just tokens.

Commoditization & Financialization
06 / 12
Market Structure
Compute is Becoming a Financial Asset. Supply Stays Structurally Scarce.
Ornn Compute and peers are building GPU market infrastructure — futures contracts, securitized hardware, standard compute units. But the ASML→TSMC→NVIDIA supply chain guarantees that the newest silicon will remain rationed to select partners and governments for the foreseeable future, sustaining a parallel broker economy that no exchange can yet replace.
The Standardization Drive
Ornn Compute and peers are attempting to define a standard compute unit (akin to a barrel of oil or MWh of electricity) that can be quoted, traded, and settled. The target: a GPU-hour equivalent that abstracts across A100/H100/H200 via a performance normalization index. The commercial goal is price discovery and forward market liquidity — matching buyers and sellers across time horizons rather than only spot.
Futures Contracts & Securitization
Hardware developers (GPU cloud operators) can securitize purchases via forward contracts: an operator buying $50M of H100s can enter a contract with an offtaker — committing to deliver X compute units at a minimum price at the end of a defined period. Offtakers (typically private credit funds) provide capital surety, backed by the contracted revenue stream. This de-risks the hardware investment cycle and enables smaller operators to access balance sheet financing that was previously only available to hyperscalers.
Price Transparency: Promise vs. Reality
Despite standardization efforts, true compute commoditization faces structural barriers that are unlikely to fully resolve. Physical configuration differences (liquid vs air cooling, rack density, power PUE) create real performance heterogeneity. Electricity cost varies 3–5× across geographies. Uptime SLAs differ dramatically. Most importantly: the software stack atop the hardware — inference engine tuning, networking optimization, support quality — contributes at least as much to real-world performance as the GPU spec sheet. A provider running SGLang with FlashAttention-3 at 85% GPU utilization offers fundamentally different value than naive hardware rental at 40% utilization — even with identical underlying silicon.
Barriers to Full Commoditization
01
Physical heterogeneity — cooling type, rack density, networking topology, power source vary enormously and translate to real performance differences
02
Software stack depth — a provider running vLLM + FlashAttention-3 at 85% utilization is not the same product as a bare metal rental; standardizing the compute unit requires abstracting this away, which is commercially and technically hard
03
Credit and counterparty risk — GPU cloud operators range from B-sheet datacenters to venture-backed startups; offtakers require credit underwriting that varies widely
04
Regulatory fragmentation — US export controls, EU data residency, and national security reviews mean that compute is increasingly jurisdiction-specific
Strategic Implications

If futures markets develop for compute, the economic logic of GPU cloud changes fundamentally: hardware operators become risk managers, not just operators. Forward sales lock in revenue; spot markets remain for opportunistic overflow. This is the electricity market model applied to silicon.

The software moat becomes more valuable in a commoditized hardware market, not less. When raw GPU-hour prices compress toward marginal cost, the only defensible margin is in the software layer sitting above it — inference optimization, model serving quality, developer tooling, and data infrastructure integration.

For a provider with both object storage and compute, the integrated offering — with zero-egress, co-located storage — becomes a genuine differentiator that a pure-play compute market cannot replicate.

The Durable Broker Market

Because NVIDIA allocates newest silicon (B200, Rubin) primarily to hyperscalers and select government partners, a large share of global compute capacity is invisible to the open market. It doesn't appear on Lambda Labs or Vast.ai — it sits in Microsoft, AWS, and Saudi Aramco clusters, accessible only through direct commercial relationships.

This creates a structurally durable role for cross-geography hardware brokers — operators with personal networks into the allocation chain who can source H200/B200 access in jurisdictions and at volumes that are simply not publicly available. This broker market persists until a marketplace or aggregator can establish live links to every entity globally with surplus, publicly-accessible compute — a problem that is commercial and political, not technical.

PagedAttention
In simple terms
When a model talks to many users at once, it needs scratch paper to track each conversation. Naive systems reserve a giant fixed pad per user — most of it wasted. PagedAttention borrows the same trick your OS uses for RAM: break it into small equal-sized pages, allocate only what's needed, share pages between users when context overlaps.
Technical detail
KV cache stored in non-contiguous fixed-size blocks (pages) via a block table — identical to OS virtual memory. Eliminates internal/external fragmentation — VRAM utilisation jumps from ~60% (naive) to near 100%. Enables request-level memory sharing: sequences sharing a prefix (system prompt, RAG context) reference the same physical KV blocks. Foundation of vLLM; critical for high-concurrency serving.
FlashAttention-3
In simple terms
Attention must compare every word to every other word — expensive because it constantly shuttles data between slow GPU storage (HBM) and fast processors. FlashAttention reorders the math to fit inside the tiny ultra-fast on-chip memory (SRAM), dramatically cutting trips to slow storage. Longer contexts become viable at practical cost.
Technical detail
Tiles Q/K/V matrices to fit in SRAM, computing attention in blocks via online softmax without materialising the full N×N matrix in HBM. FA-3 adds H100-specific optimisations: warp-specialisation, async WGMMA + SMEM pipelining, FP8 accumulation. Achieves 75% peak H100 FLOPs vs ~35% naive. Reduces HBM reads/writes from O(N²) → O(N) in the attention step. Enables 128k+ context at practical throughput.
Speculative Decoding
In simple terms
Large models generate one word at a time — always waiting. Speculative decoding adds a small cheap "draft" model that guesses several words at once. The big model checks all guesses in a single parallel pass — like a professor grading a student's draft with one glance rather than dictating word-by-word. Right guesses come for free; wrong ones are discarded.
Technical detail
Draft model (e.g. Llama 3.2 1B) autoregressively proposes K tokens. Target model runs one parallel forward pass verifying all K via rejection sampling — accepted tokens cost zero extra compute beyond the single verify step. EAGLE-2 uses a feature-level draft matching the target's hidden states: 3–4× latency speedup at >90% token acceptance. GPU utilisation improves dramatically as verification parallelises across K positions.
RadixAttention
In simple terms
If 1,000 users share the same long system prompt, naive serving computes it 1,000 times. RadixAttention treats the KV cache like a shared library — common prefixes are computed once and stored as a reusable tree. Any request that shares a prefix gets those results instantly, no re-computation. Like a shared photocopied memo rather than re-reading the original 1,000 times.
Technical detail
KV cache managed as a radix tree indexed by token sequence. Incoming requests walk the tree: matched prefix pages are reused directly (zero FLOPS), divergence triggers new KV computation and a new branch. LRU eviction manages memory under pressure. Deployed in SGLang. 4–6× throughput gain for multi-turn agentic workloads and multi-tenant deployments with shared context.
Hardware Physics: The Hard Ceiling
Transistor density improvements are slowing (Moore's Law decay). HBM bandwidth scales ~40–60% per generation — meaningful, but not the 10× leaps of the CUDA era. Memory wall, power density ceiling (500W–1kW TDP approaching datacenter limits), and interconnect latency are physical constraints that software cannot fully overcome but can navigate around via architectural innovation (MoE, speculative decoding, quantization). Hardware will keep improving — but the marginal gain per dollar shifts over time toward the software and model layers.
Value Creation Is Moving Up the Stack
As hardware matures and margins compress, the coupling between inference software quality and GPU utilization becomes the primary commercial differentiator for enterprises deploying models at scale. A provider that can sustain 85% GPU utilization, sub-50ms P99 latency, and 99.9% model uptime is not selling the same product as a provider at 40% utilization with cold-start latency — even with identical silicon. The enterprise will pay for this delta. Software performance, uptime, and utilization are the new compute moat.

How it works — full explanation
In simple terms
Imagine a hospital with 256 specialist doctors. For each patient, a triage nurse (the router) decides which 8 specialists are most relevant and calls only them in. The patient gets highly specialised care, but you only pay for 8 doctors at a time — not all 256. MoE works the same way: a lightweight router assigns each token to the best 8 of 256 expert networks. You get the knowledge of a 671B model at the cost of computing only ~37B parameters per token.
Technical
Each transformer FFN layer is replaced by N parallel expert FFN layers + a learned router. Router softmax scores tokens across N experts; top-K are activated. DeepSeek V3: 671B total, 256 experts/layer, top-8 active → 37B active params/token. Training cost ~$6M vs ~$100M for a hypothetical dense equivalent. FLOP cost ≈ dense 37B — but all 671B weights must permanently reside in HBM. This is the economic discontinuity that resets the market.
Capability Per Parameter: Smaller Models Are Getting Smarter
The relationship between parameter count and capability is no longer linear. A 2025-era 7B model, properly trained and quantized to INT4 (~4GB VRAM), routinely outperforms 2022-era 70B models on practical benchmarks. Phi-3 Mini (3.8B) exceeds GPT-3.5 on reasoning tasks. Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (INT4) fits on a phone chip and beats models that required a server cluster three years ago.
Why this is happening
Better training data curation, synthetic data pipelines, RLHF/DPO alignment, and distillation from frontier models all improve capability per parameter. More knowledge packed per byte — not more bytes. Epoch AI data: compute efficiency doubling ~every 7 months even as absolute model size plateaus.
Quantization isn't linearly destructive
FP16→INT4 drops precision per weight but capability loss is sub-linear on well-trained models. A 7B INT4 loses ~1–2% on most benchmarks vs FP16, while saving 75% VRAM. The model "knows" more than its precision suggests — what matters is the quality of training, not the representation precision at serving time.

▶ Workload Definitions — what each category actually means
Frontier Training
The initial large-scale pre-training run that builds a foundation model from scratch — ingesting trillions of tokens across weeks or months. Compute-bound and requires tightly-coupled GPU clusters (NVLink/InfiniBand fabric). Happens once per model generation. Most compute-intensive workload per job, but lowest frequency. ~12–18% of total GPU demand in 2025, declining share as inference proliferates.
Synthetic Data Gen
Using an already-trained model to generate vast quantities of labelled examples, reasoning traces, or preference pairs that are then used to train or fine-tune the next model. Feeds RLHF, DPO, and GRPO pipelines. Latency-insensitive — max throughput is the only objective. Competes directly with training for GPU allocation since both are offline batch workloads. ~10% and rising steeply as post-training becomes the primary lever for capability improvement.
Batch Inference
Serving model responses to real users and applications — the dominant category at ~50–55% of GPU demand. Covers everything from chatbot API calls to document processing pipelines. Memory-bandwidth-bound: the GPU must stream the entire weight matrix from HBM for every token generated. Throughput-optimised using continuous batching (requests queued and served in parallel) and PagedAttention. Inference volumes are doubling every ~6 months per hyperscaler disclosures.
Agentic / Multi-step
Multi-turn model pipelines where the model calls tools, browses the web, executes code, or orchestrates sub-agents — repeating inference 8–40+ times per user task. Context accumulates across turns, creating intense KV-cache memory pressure. Unlike batch inference, latency matters deeply: each step in the chain must complete before the next begins. Fastest-growing segment YoY — Anthropic, OpenAI and Google all report >3× agentic API volume growth. ~12–15% of GPU demand.
Fine-tuning / PEFT
Adapting a foundation model to a specific domain, task, or enterprise dataset — without training from scratch. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques like LoRA and QLoRA update a tiny fraction of weights (~0.1–1%), making this accessible on a single A100 or even consumer GPU. Latency-insensitive. Highly cost-elastic — enterprises run fine-tuning jobs overnight on spot instances. Grows rapidly as Llama 3, Mistral, and Qwen mature as base models. ~8–10% of GPU demand.
On-Device Inference
Model inference running entirely on end-user hardware — smartphones (Apple NPU, Snapdragon X Elite), laptops (Apple M4, Intel NPU), edge servers (Jetson Orin). No cloud GPU involved. Requires aggressive quantization: a 7B model at Q4_K_M (4-bit) fits in ~4GB DRAM and runs at 50–80 tok/s. Privacy-first by design — data never leaves the device. ~5–8% of GPU-hours but represents >40% of all inference endpoints by device count. Permanently removes commodity tasks from cloud, compressing cloud margins over time.

Strategic Thesis

Synthesis
Five Forces.
"The GPU cloud market is not a hardware rental market. It is a software-defined performance arbitrage market — and the winner owns the layer where silicon meets intelligence."
— The Compute Stack Brief · Vol. III
★ Three Opportunity Windows
① Agentic inference + integrated storage — stateful, multi-turn, long-context with KV persistence and zero-egress object storage. Not solved at production scale.
② Cross-geography hardware brokerage — network-based access to newest-generation silicon in jurisdictions and volumes unavailable on open markets; persists until a transparent aggregator exists.
③ Software utilization as a product — GPU utilization, model uptime, and inference latency sold as SLA guarantees rather than best-effort. The enterprise will pay a significant premium for operational certainty over raw FLOPS.
01
Hardware will commoditize — but the frontier stays scarce
AMD MI300X, B200 ramp, and GPU supply normalization will compress H100-tier margins by 40–60% by 2027. But the ASML→TSMC→NVIDIA supply chain structurally constrains every new generation to 18–24 months of rationed allocation. This sustains a durable market for cross-geography hardware brokers — operators with direct network access to allocations that never reach the open market — until a transparent aggregator achieves sufficient supplier coverage globally.
02
Agentic workloads require new infrastructure
Current inference servers were built for stateless single-turn. Agentic = stateful, multi-hop, KV-persistent. 8–40 model calls per task, with shared context across calls. This unsolved engineering problem is the clearest product gap in the market.
03
80% of tasks migrate on-device by 2027
Q4-quantized 30B on a $1,500 machine at conversational speed by 2026. Commodity API workloads (summarize, classify, Q&A) leave cloud at scale. The residual cloud demand is higher-value, higher-margin long-context and agentic work.
04
Sovereign AI is a durable structural force
EU AI Act, US export controls, Gulf state national AI strategies create jurisdiction-locked compute demand worth $50B+ by 2027. First-mover in each region wins decade-long enterprise contracts that hyperscalers cannot easily replicate.
05
Value creation moves up the stack — software + storage become the moat
As hardware hits physical limits (memory wall, power density ceiling, TSMC node deceleration), the marginal performance gain per dollar migrates to the software layer above it. GPU utilization, model uptime, and inference latency — not raw FLOPS — become the enterprise buying criteria. The provider coupling best-in-class inference software with co-located, zero-egress object storage owns the only defensible margin left when silicon commoditizes.
