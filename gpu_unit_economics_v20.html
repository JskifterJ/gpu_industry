⚡ GPU Infrastructure Economics

Model the unit economics of GPU cluster financing — from CAPEX through revenue, P&L, and investor returns. Defaults are set to a realistic baseline: 8× H100 nodes, 55% LTV bank financing, SPV with a 70% investor pool.
1
Configure the sidebar
Pick your GPU, toggle New vs Refurbished (adjusts price and AFR automatically), set fleet size, choose demand mode (offtake / spot / blended), and adjust financing terms. The SPV / investor pool is off by default — toggle it on in the sidebar to model a preferred/junior equity split between pool investors and the operator.
2
Read the fund flow diagram
The illustration at the top shows how money moves between Lender, Operator, Pool Investors, the GPU Project, and Customer — with live amounts on every arrow.
3
Check the KPI strip
CAPEX, EBITDA margin, equity ROI, break-even price, payback month, and DSCR update on every change. The break-even price shows the minimum $/hr needed to cover your equity cost.
4
Inspect the P&L
Five numbered blocks: ① accounting P&L (EBIT), ② operating cash flow, ③ terminal / exit, ④ equity return (with pool split), ⑤ NPV & IRR. Numbers always reconcile — pool + operator = total equity CF.
5
Run sensitivity analysis
Table 1: price × contract duration → ROI. Table 2: LTV × interest rate → ROI. Toggle NPV mode for risk-adjusted heatmaps. Current assumption is outlined in each table.
6
Compare financing scenarios
The Financing tab shows 4 scenarios side-by-side: bank IO, ABS amortizing, token-collateral vault, and self-finance — all with the same operating assumptions.
Baseline defaults: 8 nodes × 8 GPUs (H100 SXM5, $32k/card new) · $2.00/hr offtake · 55% LTV @ 10% IO · SPV / investor pool off by default · New cards selected (4% AFR) — toggle to Refurbished for ~45% lower price, ~9% AFR · 24-month contract · 50% residual · 1mo ramp-up delay
Note: Per Ornn Compute, GPUs depreciate 40–70% over 24mo. The residual price floor can be secured via a Residual Value Swap (RVS) with Ornn Compute — this reduces lender risk, enabling lower interest rates and higher LTV. See the residual slider in advanced settings.

Why does scenario B (ABS amortizing) look low?
Amortizing loans require monthly principal repayments during the contract — this cash leaves the operator immediately rather than sitting as equity. IO debt note loans only pay interest monthly and return the bulk at exit (bullet). So amortizing cumulative ops cashflow is always lower than IO, even at the same LTV. The difference is timing: amortizing equity return is spread across the contract; IO is concentrated at exit. Both are valid — check the equity ROI chart for the true comparison.
