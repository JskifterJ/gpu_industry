# **The New Petro-State is a Data Center: Geopolitics, Financialization, and the Future of Compute Trading**

## **Introduction: The Structural Transition from Crude to Compute**

In the architecture of the global economy, foundational commodities dictate the distribution of geopolitical power, the structure of capital markets, and the velocity of technological advancement. Throughout the twentieth century, crude oil served as this foundational input. The extraction, refinement, and logistical distribution of hydrocarbons defined national sovereignty, triggered global conflicts, and created the modern financial system.1 In the twenty-first century, a profound structural transition has occurred: computational power—specifically the nexus of advanced semiconductor hardware, massive data-processing infrastructure, and localized gigawatt-scale energy generation—has emerged as the defining strategic resource.1 "Compute" is no longer merely a background technological service; it is the primary lever of technological, economic, and military sovereignty, assuming the historical mantle of oil and steel.1

The parallels between traditional petrochemical commodities and advanced computational hardware are striking. Both require massive, capital-intensive upfront investments that demand sustained coordination among private firms, state actors, and apex financial institutions.3 Both resources are inherently dual-use, underpinning civilian economic productivity while simultaneously enabling modern military and intelligence capabilities, rendering their supply chains a matter of acute national security.1 Furthermore, both markets are subject to stringent export controls, regulatory pressures, and the constant threat of intentional disruption through geopolitical sanctions.3

However, critical divergences exist that make the compute market uniquely complex, requiring novel trading strategies. Unlike crude oil, which is a highly fungible and physically durable commodity that can be stored in strategic reserves indefinitely, compute is driven by silicon that undergoes rapid technological depreciation.5 While a barrel of Brent crude remains fundamentally identical over decades, an enterprise-grade Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) faces technological obsolescence within a matter of years due to relentless architectural innovation.7 Additionally, while oil distribution relies on maritime chokepoints and physical pipelines, compute relies on a highly concentrated supply chain of semiconductor fabrication plants and the physical constraints of energy-dense hyperscale data centers.3

As the artificial intelligence (AI) economy demands unprecedented computational capacity, the market structure surrounding GPUs is rapidly evolving into a mature commodity ecosystem. The emergence of secondary hardware markets, asset-backed debt financing, spot-pricing indices, and compute-linked derivatives signals the rapid financialization of this resource.9 Understanding this landscape requires synthesizing the geopolitical strategies of nation-states with the operational models of traditional commodity trading houses as chronicled by Javier Blas in *The World for Sale*.13 By applying the historical lessons of the global oil trade to the modern silicon supply chain, market participants—such as specialized compute trading platforms—can identify distinct opportunities for arbitrage, infrastructure financing, and strategic capacity brokering.

## **The Geopolitics of Sovereign AI: Echoes of Oil Nationalization**

The realization that computational power dictates future economic viability has triggered a global race for "Sovereign AI"—the capability of a nation to produce and control artificial intelligence using its own infrastructure, data, and domestic workforce.15 This modern imperative closely mirrors the wave of oil industry nationalization in the 1970s. During that era, states such as Libya and Venezuela recognized that leaving critical resources entirely in the hands of foreign private multinationals posed an unacceptable strategic vulnerability, prompting the creation of powerful state-owned enterprises like Petróleos de Venezuela (PDVSA).16 Today, states are actively intervening in the compute market to secure capacity, establish domestic data centers, and control the flow of advanced chips to ensure they are not beholden to foreign tech monopolies.

### **The GCC AI Stack and the Pivot from Hydrocarbons**

The Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) member states, particularly the United Arab Emirates (UAE) and Saudi Arabia, are at the vanguard of this sovereign transition. These nations are deliberately leveraging their historical advantages in energy abundance and sovereign wealth to pivot their economies from "crude to compute".2 Unlike energy-constrained Western nations, the Gulf states possess vast hydrocarbon reserves, rapidly growing renewable energy portfolios, and the centralized capital necessary to fund gigawatt-scale data center infrastructure without straining public electrical grids.2

To avoid the strategic vulnerability of relying entirely on Western cloud providers, these nations are building "Strategic Compute Reserves".17 This involves the aggressive, state-backed procurement of tens of thousands of advanced GPUs. For example, the Saudi sovereign wealth fund, the Public Investment Fund (PIF), backed the national AI firm Humain to secure advanced Blackwell chips (GB300s) and develop 500 megawatts of domestic AI data centers, marking a $10 billion push into domestic infrastructure.19 Similarly, the UAE has positioned G42 and the state-backed MGX investment firm to build massive AI infrastructure, participating as key capital providers in global initiatives like the proposed $500 billion Stargate Project.19

These sovereign AI initiatives operate with the same mandate and market weight as national oil companies (NOCs) like Saudi Aramco.21 They are heavily capitalized, directly aligned with state strategic goals (such as Saudi Vision 2030), and are designed to exert geopolitical influence by controlling a vital resource.20 The Gulf's ambition is to become a "trusted AI node" and the prime backend provider of "compute-as-a-service" for emerging markets across Africa and Asia, effectively acting as the central distribution hub for computational power in the Global South.2

## **The Weaponization of the Supply Chain: Export Controls and Trade Routes**

Just as oil embargoes have historically been utilized as potent instruments of economic warfare, the flow of advanced GPUs is currently being strictly controlled by state actors to maintain technological superiority and delay the advancement of geopolitical rivals. The United States has aggressively weaponized its position at the apex of semiconductor design to restrict rival access to frontier compute capabilities, creating immense friction in the global trading of these assets.15

### **The Architecture of Silicon Sanctions**

The regulatory landscape surrounding compute has been highly volatile, shifting from broad prohibitions to targeted, transactional diplomacy. Under the Biden administration, the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) implemented a "small yard, high fence" approach, deploying the Foreign Direct Product Rule (FDPR) to restrict the export of advanced AI chips and semiconductor manufacturing equipment to China and other nations of concern.22 This included the January 2025 AI Diffusion Framework, which established strict "Know Your Customer" (KYC) requirements to prevent adversaries from accessing compute via remote cloud instances and extended controls to the actual AI model weights.22

By early 2026, the licensing review policy for advanced computing commodities evolved to define precise technical thresholds. To be eligible for case-by-case export review to regions like China or Macau, chips must possess a Total Processing Performance (TPP) of less than 21,000 and a total DRAM bandwidth of less than 6,500 GB/s.25 Furthermore, exporters must prove that these shipments do not compromise domestic U.S. supply and must submit hardware to independent third-party testing labs prior to export.25

### **Unintended Consequences and Market Distortions**

Unilateral export controls often create market distortions and incentivize the rapid development of indigenous alternatives by restricted nations, a dynamic heavily observed during historical commodity embargoes.23 In response to strict controls, China accelerated its domestic semiconductor ecosystem, attempting to bypass Western chokepoints through alternative architectures and massive state investment.27 Concurrently, a robust illicit market emerged. Smuggling operations, utilizing shell companies and complex transshipment routes through Southeast Asia, successfully moved hundreds of millions of dollars worth of restricted Nvidia GPUs into Chinese data centers.27

Recognizing the limitations of pure embargoes and the risk of losing global market share, U.S. policy shifted in late 2025 and early 2026 toward an "American AI Exports Program".25 This strategy permits the export of the comprehensive "American AI Technology Stack"—including hardware, cloud infrastructure, and software—to allied nations, provided they accept rigorous security audits, continuous monitoring, and strict limits on Chinese collaboration.25

For market participants, these geopolitical interventions generate profound volatility. Compute trading platforms must navigate a labyrinth of end-user controls, diversion risks, and shifting licensing requirements. Managing compliance and proving the provenance of hardware and the identity of end-users is now as critical to compute trading as sanctions compliance and maritime tracking are to the global oil trade.25

## **The Supplier Oligopoly: The New "Seven Sisters"**

The global oil market is heavily influenced by the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) and was historically dominated by the "Seven Sisters"—a cartel of multinational oil companies that controlled extraction, refining, and distribution in the mid-twentieth century.34 The modern compute market exhibits an even more extreme degree of supplier concentration, rendering it highly vulnerable to supply shocks, pricing monopolies, and centralized control.

### **Nvidia's Unprecedented Market Dominance**

The market for advanced AI accelerators is overwhelmingly dominated by a single entity: Nvidia. Throughout 2024 and 2025, Nvidia consistently captured between 86% and 92% of the discrete GPU and data center accelerator market share.36 This dominance is not merely a function of producing superior silicon hardware; it is deeply entrenched by Nvidia's proprietary Compute Unified Device Architecture (CUDA) software ecosystem. CUDA has become the undisputed industry standard for AI developers, creating massive switching costs for consumers and serving as a virtually impenetrable economic moat.37

While competitors are aggressively attempting to capture market share—with AMD securing roughly 8% of the discrete GPU market and forging major infrastructure partnerships, and Intel attempting to pivot its legacy dominance into AI—Nvidia's absolute market power allows it to dictate pricing, allocate supply selectively, and enforce stringent contractual terms.36 This dynamic creates a market where access to the commodity is highly permissioned. Primary market allocation is often based on strategic relationships, geopolitical alignment, and purchasing scale rather than pure price discovery.

### **Physical and Logistical Chokepoints**

While global oil flows are vulnerable to maritime transit chokepoints such as the Strait of Hormuz, the Suez Canal, and the Malacca Strait 41, the flow of compute is constrained by highly localized technological and physical bottlenecks.

First, the actual fabrication of these advanced chips is overwhelmingly reliant on the Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) and the extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines produced exclusively by the Dutch firm ASML.34 The geographic concentration of leading-edge semiconductor manufacturing in Taiwan represents a critical single point of failure for the global compute supply chain. A geopolitical crisis in the Taiwan Strait would halt the production of the world's most critical economic input instantly, creating a supply shock that would dwarf the 1970s oil crises.35

Second, the deployment of compute is physically constrained by data center capacity, power availability, and high-bandwidth fiber-optic connectivity.43 Modern AI workloads require hyperscale data centers with power densities far exceeding traditional enterprise IT infrastructure. A massive GPU cluster capable of training frontier models can require upwards of 150 megawatts of continuous power, placing unprecedented strain on local electrical grids.46

The logistical challenge of compute trading is fundamentally tied to this "energy-compute nexus." Finding the physical real estate, securing gigawatt-scale energy contracts (often requiring dedicated renewable generation or associated natural gas projects), and managing advanced liquid cooling systems are the modern equivalents of building complex oil refineries and deep-water shipping terminals.20 Compute cannot exist in a vacuum; a naked GPU is useless without the power and infrastructure to host it.

## **The Asset Lifecycle: GPU Depreciation vs. Oil Fungibility**

A fundamental structural divergence between traditional commodities and compute lies in the nature of the asset's lifespan. Crude oil is perfectly fungible and durable; a barrel stored for five years retains its exact chemical composition, energy density, and economic utility.6 In contrast, a GPU is a rapidly depreciating technological asset. The relentless pace of hardware innovation means that a top-tier chip today will be technologically outclassed by a new generation within 12 to 18 months.6 For a compute trader, hoarding the physical asset is detrimental; GPUs must be continuously utilized to extract value before they decay technologically.

### **The Great Recalculation and the "6-Year Shift"**

Historically, cloud hyperscalers (Amazon Web Services, Google Cloud, and Microsoft Azure) depreciated standard server assets over a three- to four-year timeline.5 However, as capital expenditures for AI infrastructure swelled into the hundreds of billions, a coordinated financial maneuver occurred across the industry: the "6-Year Shift".5 By extending the official accounting useful life of server and networking assets to six years, hyperscalers significantly reduced their annual depreciation expenses. This accounting change generated multi-billion-dollar tailwinds to their GAAP operating income, masking the true cash-burn of the AI buildout.5 For example, Amazon's shift to a longer depreciation schedule added roughly $3.2 billion to its operating income in a single year.5

Critics argue that this accounting posture is aggressively optimistic, pointing to the rapid obsolescence of AI hardware. For instance, Nvidia's transition from the Hopper (H100) architecture to the Blackwell (B200/B300) architecture offers massive multiples of performance-per-watt.7 In a power-constrained data center, running older chips that consume the same megawattage but produce a fraction of the computational output becomes economically unviable.7

### **The "Value Cascade" Framework**

Despite this rapid architectural turnover, deep market analysis reveals that GPUs possess a durable economic lifespan governed by a "Value Cascade".5 Rather than becoming instantly obsolete, aging GPUs transition through a hierarchy of workloads, maximizing the total revenue-per-token over a five- to six-year physical lifespan:

1. **Years 1–2 (Primary Economic Life):** Cutting-edge GPUs are deployed exclusively for **Foundational Model Training**. This phase demands absolute peak performance, interconnect bandwidth, and massive parallel processing. Only the newest, most expensive silicon is viable for organizations racing to achieve state-of-the-art model capabilities.5  
2. **Years 3–4 (Secondary Economic Life):** As next-generation silicon assumes the training workloads, the older chips cascade down to **Premium Real-Time Inference**. Inference—the process of querying an already-trained model in production—is highly profitable but less sensitive to the extreme performance and networking constraints of training. The insatiable global demand for AI deployment easily absorbs this "last-gen" capacity.5  
3. **Years 5–6 (Tertiary Economic Life):** Fully depreciated assets enter their final profitable phase, handling **Batch Inference, Analytics, and Internal Utility Workloads**. These tasks, such as fine-tuning, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and asynchronous data processing, are not latency-sensitive and can be executed highly efficiently on older architectures.5

It is crucial to note that identical hardware can age differently based on its operational history. A GPU running steady inference at 60–70% capacity under controlled thermals degrades much slower than one subjected to the extreme power spikes and thermal limits of continuous model training.6 Therefore, for a compute trading house, tracking hardware telemetry, thermal stress history, and precise utilization profiles is exactly analogous to assaying the chemical purity of a physical ore shipment.6

## **The World for Sale: Applying the Commodity Trading House Model**

In his seminal work *The World for Sale*, Javier Blas details how independent commodity trading houses—such as Vitol, Trafigura, and Glencore—amassed enormous power and wealth by acting as the vital connective tissue of the global economy.13 These firms did not necessarily extract or consume commodities; rather, they exploited information asymmetries, mastered complex physical logistics, navigated high-risk geopolitical environments, and provided critical liquidity and trade credit to market participants.13

This precise business model is currently being replicated in the AI infrastructure space. Because compute capacity is scarce, geographically constrained, and heavily controlled by a few massive hyperscalers, a new ecosystem of specialized brokers, "neoclouds," and decentralized marketplaces has emerged to clear the market. An organization like ICN can strategically position itself within this ecosystem by mirroring the operations of the great commodity houses.

### **Exploiting Information Asymmetry in Secondary Markets**

Commodity traders generate outsized profits from information asymmetry—knowing exactly where a resource is undervalued, where it is desperately needed, and the true cost of moving it.53 In the compute market, information asymmetry manifests in opaque hyperscaler pricing, hidden hardware degradation, and fragmented secondary market availability.6

A highly lucrative secondary market for used enterprise GPUs is rapidly developing. Hardware from 2022 and 2023 contract expirations (e.g., lightly used H100s) frequently re-enters the market. Surprisingly, these assets have been shown to retain up to 95% of their original pricing upon rebooking due to sustained inference demand outstripping new supply.57 However, navigating this secondary market is fraught with severe risks. Deep discounts on the "grey market" (platforms like eBay or unauthorized brokers) are offset by the threat of voided warranties, firmware-locked hardware, or components that have suffered severe thermal degradation during training workloads.57

A sophisticated compute trading house manages this asymmetry through rigorous verification. By partnering with Information Technology Asset Disposition (ITAD) firms and certified remarketing channels, the trader verifies hardware provenance, ensures secure data destruction, and conducts functional stress-testing.57 By resolving the "trust gap" in the secondary market, the trader can acquire discounted wholesale hardware and lease it at premium retail rates to startups requiring inference capacity.

### **Logistics, Bundling, and the Neocloud Model**

"Neoclouds"—specialized, AI-native cloud providers such as CoreWeave and Lambda Labs—have stepped into the market void left by general-purpose hyperscalers.5 These firms operate much like physical commodity traders: they secure massive allocations of underlying raw materials (Nvidia GPUs), build out the necessary holding infrastructure (power-dense data centers), and lease that capacity to end-users at a premium.60

For a platform offering compute sourcing, managing logistics is paramount. Successful sellers manage "remarketing complexity" by offering complete, turnkey GPU servers rather than parting out individual chips.57 This simplifies deployment for the buyer and often yields higher returns for the seller. Furthermore, platforms like SF Compute, Dataoorts, and Thunder Compute act as dynamic spot markets and clearinghouses.56 They match buyers seeking rapid, flexible access to bare-metal compute with sellers holding excess or idle capacity.56 This unbundling of compute provides vital flexibility for researchers who require short-term access without committing to the draconian, multi-year Service Level Agreements (SLAs) demanded by incumbent clouds.56

### **Decentralized Infrastructure and Web3 Marketplaces**

Pushing the commodity trading model further, decentralized GPU networks like Aethir and Render Network aim to aggregate idle consumer and enterprise hardware globally, functioning like a distributed cooperative of wildcat oil drillers.67 By utilizing tokenized incentives and blockchain ledgers to coordinate trustless supply and demand, these platforms create massive, distributed compute pools.67 For instance, Aethir operates a "Strategic Compute Reserve" that deploys hundreds of thousands of distributed GPU containers to service enterprise workloads, bridging the gap between decentralized Web3 supply and compliant Web2 corporate demand.18

## **The Financialization of Compute: Debt, Derivatives, and Hedging**

The ultimate hallmark of a maturing commodity market is financialization: the transition from trading the physical asset to trading financial instruments derived from that asset. Historically, the introduction of oil futures in the 1980s revolutionized energy markets, allowing producers to hedge risk and speculators to provide liquidity without ever taking delivery of a physical barrel.13 In the mid-2020s, the compute market explicitly crossed this threshold.

### **Asset-Backed Private Credit and GPU Financing**

The capital expenditure required to build AI factories is staggering, with estimates suggesting up to $7 trillion will be needed worldwide by 2030\.10 This immense scale of capital cannot be entirely absorbed by corporate equity or traditional balance-sheet debt, particularly for rapidly scaling startups burning billions in R\&D.9

Consequently, the market has witnessed a massive influx of asset-backed private credit, where the physical GPUs themselves serve as collateral for multi-billion-dollar loans.9 Alternative asset managers and private equity titans—including Blackstone, Apollo Global Management, and Magnetar—have stepped in to underwrite historic debt facilities.9

Notable transactions illustrate this paradigm shift:

* **CoreWeave Debt Facilities:** The AI neocloud secured a $7.5 billion debt financing facility led by Blackstone, following a $2.3 billion facility the previous year. The capital is explicitly earmarked to purchase GPU hardware to fulfill already-executed client contracts, essentially utilizing future lease receivables and the underlying silicon as security.61  
* **Apollo and xAI:** Apollo Global Management orchestrated roughly $7 billion in financing across two major deals for Elon Musk's xAI. Utilizing Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs) and triple-net lease structures managed by Valor Equity Partners, investment vehicles purchase the Nvidia chips and lease them to xAI. This structure removes the massive capital expenditure from xAI's direct corporate balance sheet while providing Apollo's investors with steady yield backed by the hardware's residual value.9

This off-balance-sheet financing relies heavily on the assumption that GPUs retain significant residual value over time.10 Lenders must meticulously model the "Value Cascade" and depreciation curves to ensure that their collateral outlasts the loan term.6 The willingness of apex financial institutions to lend against silicon confirms that GPUs are no longer viewed merely as IT expenses, but as durable, revenue-generating hard assets.

### **Price Discovery: Spot Indices and Compute Derivatives**

A persistent challenge in the compute market has been the lack of a transparent clearing price. As noted by industry observers, securing compute traditionally involves negotiating opaque SLAs with cloud providers, with "no ticker, no clearing price, no easy way to comparison-shop".56 Commodity trading relies on standardized pricing to facilitate hedging and risk transfer.

To solve this, financial infrastructure firms are building the tools necessary for a liquid compute market. **Ornn Data** has emerged as a critical benchmark provider, creating pricing indices (such as the OCPI-H100 and OCPI-B200) based on real, live spot-market transaction data rather than estimated quotes or scraped offers.12 This transaction-based index provides the definitive reference for the spot price of compute, finally establishing a transparent market clearing rate.75

Building upon these indices, **Architect Financial Technologies** launched the first exchange-traded perpetual futures contracts linked directly to AI compute and memory pricing.11 Traded on a regulated institutional exchange, these derivatives allow data center operators, AI labs, and hardware vendors to hedge against the severe price volatility of GPU rentals.11 Just as airlines use futures to hedge against jet fuel spikes, AI companies can now lock in the future cost of their computational power. This financial layer transforms compute from a purely operational challenge into a tradeable, hedgeable commodity class, unlocking advanced trading strategies.76

## **Strategic Positioning for ICN: The Compute Trading House Blueprint**

For an organization like ICN aiming to position itself as a compute trading house or an infrastructure brokering platform, the intersection of geopolitical constraints, hardware lifecycles, and financialization presents a generational opportunity. Success in this emerging sector requires synthesizing multiple competencies drawn directly from the historical playbooks of Vitol, Trafigura, and Glencore, adapted for the realities of silicon and power.

### **1\. Arbitraging the Value Cascade and Secondary Markets**

A successful compute trader must not view a GPU as a static asset, but as a dynamic yield-generating instrument. ICN should focus on aggressively sourcing hardware at the mid-point of its lifecycle. By purchasing lightly used H100s as hyperscalers upgrade to Blackwell, ICN can offer highly competitive rates for premium inference workloads.49 The core arbitrage lies in matching the specific architectural capability of the hardware to the exact latency and precision requirements of the client's workload.50 Developing rigorous in-house telemetry and stress-testing protocols will allow ICN to safely navigate the grey market, mitigating the severe information asymmetry that plagues secondary GPU sales.57

### **2\. Mastering the "Power-to-Compute" Nexus**

In the modern compute economy, access to silicon is only half the equation; the true physical bottleneck is gigawatt-scale power.44 A trading house must also function as an energy logistics firm. ICN can generate massive alpha by identifying regions with structural energy surpluses. Co-locating data center capacity near stranded or associated natural gas, leveraging renewable energy credits, and optimizing cooling efficiency (Power Usage Effectiveness or PUE) directly lowers the marginal cost of compute.47 The spread between localized, cheap energy costs and global, standardized compute rental rates represents the primary source of physical trading profit.

### **3\. Providing Market Liquidity and Financial Hedging**

AI startups and mid-market enterprises face existential risks from compute price volatility. By utilizing platforms like Architect and indices from Ornn Data, ICN can offer clients fixed-price forward contracts for compute capacity, absorbing the underlying spot-market volatility.11 ICN could act as a market maker, leasing raw capacity on a floating rate while offering fixed-rate SLAs to end-users, hedging the exposure through compute derivatives. Providing this credit and risk-management function is exactly how historical commodity houses locked in long-term client loyalty.51

### **4\. Navigating Geopolitical Fences and Trade Routes**

In a market defined by state intervention, regulatory compliance is a massive competitive advantage. ICN must implement rigorous KYC, end-user verification, and telemetry tracking to ensure that compute capacity is not diverted to sanctioned entities.22 Firms that can seamlessly operate within the strict bounds of the "American AI Exports Program" while servicing the massive, state-backed demand of the GCC and the Global South will capture significant geographic arbitrage.25 Understanding exactly which compute tiers (e.g., TPP under 21,000) can flow to specific jurisdictions allows the trader to legally clear markets that competitors avoid due to regulatory fear.25

## **Conclusion**

The evolution of the artificial intelligence economy has irrevocably transformed high-performance computational hardware from a niche IT component into a foundational global commodity. The parallels to the twentieth-century oil market are profound: nation-states are hoarding capacity as a matter of sovereign security, a tightly concentrated oligopoly of suppliers dictates market terms, and capital markets are inventing novel financial structures to fund unprecedented infrastructure expansion.

Yet, compute is a commodity defined by rapid technological decay and absolute dependence on electrical power. Market participants seeking to operate as modern "compute trading houses" must navigate a landscape far more complex than moving physical barrels of crude. They must master the "Value Cascade" to extract maximum economic utility from depreciating silicon, secure vast reserves of localized energy, and utilize emerging derivatives markets to hedge against extreme price volatility.

Just as the pioneering commodity traders of the late twentieth century built immense empires by operating in the opaque, high-friction seams of the global supply chain, the titans of the twenty-first-century economy will be those who successfully finance, broker, and deliver the computational power that fuels the AI revolution. For platforms like ICN, the financialization of compute is no longer a theoretical horizon; it is the active, rapidly maturing reality of the global market, ripe for sophisticated intermediation and trade.
